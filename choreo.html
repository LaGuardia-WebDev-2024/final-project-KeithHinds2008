Choreographers are both artists and storytellers. They use movement to create meaning, blending abstract ideas with clear messages. Drawing from music, theater, fashion, and politics, choreography becomes a powerful way to express emotion, social issues, and visual beauty. It’s a living form of memory, shaped by the body’s strength, struggles, and spirit. At its heart, choreography asks: how can the body say what words cannot? How can movement hold memories or shape identity?

Two choreographers who have deeply influenced my understanding of dance are Camille A. Brown and Parris Goebel.

Camille A. Brown’s work connects dance with history and storytelling. Her piece City of Rain, a tribute to a lost friend, is a moving example of how dance can honor memory. Through expressive group movement and emotional gestures, she turns grief into beauty. Her choreography often explores African-American identity and cultural history, using everyday movement to tell powerful, poetic stories.

Parris Goebel, on the other hand, is known for her bold, high-energy commercial style. She mixes street dance with drama and storytelling, creating performances that are both visually striking and emotionally powerful. She’s worked with stars like Rihanna, Justin Bieber, and Jennifer Lopez. Her work on Rihanna’s Savage X Fenty shows blends fashion, choreography, and diverse body representation, turning the runway into an inclusive and empowering performance space.

Together, Brown and Goebel show the wide range of what choreography can be — from deeply personal and cultural storytelling to groundbreaking commercial spectacle. Both use dance to challenge norms and celebrate identity.

They’ve helped shape my own choreographic voice. I’ve come to see that humanity and connection are essential. My style is rooted in contemporary dance, but I also draw from ballet, jazz, and vogue/ballroom, creating a blend that speaks to emotion, community, and shared experience.
